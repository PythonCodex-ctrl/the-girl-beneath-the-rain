The Girl Beneath the Rain

Some people become memories. Others become stories....

Approx. 18 minutes read

"Some stories aren't written with ink. They are written by the people who change us."

Chapter One

The Stranger Next Door

There is a particular kind of quiet that follows graduation. Not the peaceful kind. The other kind — the one that settles inside your chest like an unfinished sentence that refuses to end.

Joseph, a 26-year-old graduate of Biochemistry, knew that quiet well.

He had lived inside it for the better part of the months following the day he collected his degree certificate, shook a few hands, and walked out of the university that had shaped six years of his life, only to discover that the world beyond its gates had no idea what to do with a young man carrying a degree in Biochemistry and a heart full of questions about whether he had chosen the right path at all.

He was tall—not excessively so, but tall enough that doorframes occasionally felt personal. His mind moved quickly, and his hands had, over the previous year, grown more familiar with Petri dishes and spectrophotometers than almost anything else they touched.

He had drifted into tech almost by accident, the way many of life's defining moments begin — not with a dramatic decision, but with a quiet, persistent inclination. A friend had needed help unlocking and flashing a device. Joseph had helped. He enjoyed it more than he expected and he has always had the curious mindset of wanting to know how things worked and functioned whether biologically, chemically or even technically.

So he kept going...

Researches were made into the area of tech to venture into. He had researched, verified and grouped according to their difficulty level, earnings, freelance-friendliness, and other options before he finally settled for a career in Backend Development.

Yet none of it settled the uncertainty.

None of it answered the larger question that accompanied him through long nights.

"What exactly are you building? And where, exactly, are you going?"

So he did what many young men do when life feels too vast and too undefined.

He made a list of things he hopes to achieve before the year runs out and then proceeded to move in with family while he figured things out.

His cousin lived in a modest but welcoming compound in a neighbourhood suspended somewhere between the restless energy of the city and the calm of its outskirts. It was the sort of place where neighbours still greeted one another, where the creak of a gate carried through the evening air, and where mornings drifted in with the comforting aroma of breakfast being prepared somewhere nearby.

Joseph arrived carrying two bags, a laptop, and the quiet determination of someone who knew he was meant for something more but had not yet discovered what shape that "more" would take.

His cousin welcomed him the way good family always does — with teasing, warm food, and no expectation that he explain himself before he was ready.

The compound had a small sitting area overlooking the street, and Joseph soon made it his evening refuge. There, with his laptop balanced across his knees, he worked through online courses, coding exercises, and backend development assignments while the sky slowly surrendered its amber glow to deep shades of purple and navy.

It was on one such evening, perhaps a week or two after his arrival, that he saw her for the first time.

She was walking home after having her hair done.

She moved with the kind of effortless energy that made it seem as though even the pavement welcomed her footsteps. She was smiling at something on her phone when, almost instinctively, she lifted her head.

Their eyes met.

Joseph looked away immediately.

The way you do when you see something you are unsure about.

Still, he had seen enough.

She was young, about five feet seven inches tall, bright-eyed, with a smile that somehow seemed to arrive a heartbeat before the rest of her face and a gap between her teeth.

A moment later, she disappeared through the gate of a compound a few houses away from his cousin's.

Joseph told himself he didn't think about her again that evening, even though he asked his cousin about her later that night.

Whether or not that was true was another matter entirely.

Chapter Two

When Two Worlds Met

What Joseph could not have known, as he watched her disappear through that gate, was that she carried far more within her than her easy smile suggested.

Her name was Cynthia.

She was twenty-two years old and had recently received the news she had spent much of her adolescence working towards: an admission to study Music at a prestigious university within the country.

The admission letter had arrived three weeks before Joseph moved into the neighbourhood. She had read it four times before allowing herself to believe it. Then she cried. Then she laughed. Then she made a list.

Aria loved lists.

She loved plans, questions, and the quiet satisfaction of understanding something she had once found confusing. She loved learning the way some people loved food — not out of necessity, but out of genuine, insatiable hunger. She could lose an entire afternoon reading biographies, novels, or books on music theory, only to wonder where the hours had gone.

She dreamed bigger than most people expected.

Not loudly. Never arrogantly.

She carried her ambitions carefully, the way one carries something precious that must not be dropped. She spoke about becoming a musician not with exaggerated confidence but with the quiet certainty of someone who had already imagined the stages she hoped to stand on, the songs she longed to write, and the lives she dreamed of touching through her music.

She was an easy-going, cheerful, and playful person.

That was perhaps the first thing anyone noticed about her.

Before people discovered her ambitions or understood the depth of her character, they encountered her brightness. She laughed easily. She teased without cruelty. She possessed the rare ability to turn even the most ordinary moments into stories worth telling later.

Her joy was never performative.

It was simply the natural climate of her personality.

Yet there was a side of her very few people ever saw.

About a year before Joseph moved into the neighbourhood, a relationship had quietly come to an end. It was her second relationship, and like the first, it had lasted only a few months.

There had been no betrayal.

No dramatic confrontation.

No spectacular heartbreak.

Only the slow, painful realization that two people could care for one another and still be building entirely different futures.

She had recognized it first.

She had been the last to admit it aloud.

For a month, she allowed herself to grieve.

Then, true to herself, she made another list.

Not a list of regrets.

A list of promises.

Promises about the woman she hoped to become.

Promises to protect her peace.

Promises to never lose herself in loving someone else again.

By the time the stranger on the neighbouring veranda caught her attention, she had healed — or at least healed enough that only she could still recognize the faint traces of the wound.

She was not searching for love.

She was looking ahead.

She had a degree in Music to earn, countless hours of practice ahead of her, and dreams that demanded every bit of dedication she could offer.

She noticed the young man on the veranda.

He had looked away so quickly that she couldn't help smiling to herself in a way that revealed her white, well arranged, gapped dentures.

She found it quietly amusing.

Still, she was far too shy to approach him.

So she simply walked through her gate and continued with the rest of her evening.

Or at least...

that was the version of the story she would later insist was true.

Chapter Three

The Language of Quiet Moments

It began, as most beautiful things do, without intention or any form of planning.

One afternoon, Joseph walked to the corner shop at the end of the street. His cousin had asked him to buy a few groceries, and he was waiting at the counter when she walked in behind him, already laughing at something the shopkeeper had said before she had even reached the entrance.

He stepped aside to let her pass.

"Thank you," she said with a smile.

"It's nothing," he replied.

She looked at him for a brief moment, her expression thoughtful, as though she were trying to place a face she had seen somewhere before.

"You live in Philips' compound, don't you?" she asked.

"My cousin's compound actually, Philips' his friend.." Joseph corrected gently.

She nodded.

"Ah... so you're the new neighbour."

"I wouldn't say I'm new here considering the fact I've been here almost two weeks already."

The conversation should have ended there.

Instead, it grew.

She asked where he had lived before moving into the neighbourhood. He told her about the town where he attended university. She mentioned that one of her aunts had once lived nearby. He admitted he hadn't explored that part of town very much. She laughed and confessed that she hadn't visited it in years.

By the time they paid for their groceries, they were still talking.

They continued walking together.

They reached the space between their two compounds.

Still, neither of them seemed eager to leave.

Finally, Joseph smiled.

"The name's Joseph," he said, extending his hand. "It's a pleasure meeting you."

She looked at his hand for a second before taking it.

"I'm Cynthia."

There was something unexpectedly formal about shaking hands after such an effortless conversation.

Years later, they would laugh about it.

After that afternoon, conversation became a habit.

Most mornings, Cynthia would leave the house for a walk just as Joseph happened to be sitting on the veranda either with his laptop, or doom-scrolling through his phone or just recieving the cool morning breeze. She would comment on the weather, ask what he was working on, or what his doing, or just tease him for looking too serious.

Minutes would disappear before either of them noticed.

Some evenings, Joseph would step outside to clear his head after hours of coding, only to find Cynthia sitting on the low wall beside the road, humming a melody she had been practising or reading lyrics she'd scribbled into a notebook.

She always looked up when she heard him.

He always found a reason to stay.

They spoke about everything that felt safe, and gradually, about the things that didn't.

Cynthia told him about her admission to study Music and the dreams she carried with it. Though she spoke humbly, Joseph could hear the quiet determination beneath every word.

Joseph told her about studying Biochemistry before discovering an unexpected love for technology specifically technology about computers. He explained how curiosity had slowly become purpose, and purpose had become a new direction.

Unlike most people, Cynthia didn't simply nod politely.

She asked questions.

Real questions.

Questions that forced him to explain why he enjoyed programming, why solving problems excited him, and why he believed technology could change lives.

To his surprise, he found himself answering with a clarity he had never managed before.

It was as though she was helping him understand his own journey simply by listening.

She made him laugh.

That surprised him most of all.

After six years of keeping largely to himself, he had almost forgotten what it felt like to laugh because of one particular person — not because of something he had seen online, but because someone sitting across from him had looked at the world from an unexpected angle and said exactly the right thing.

Without noticing when it happened, Joseph began looking forward to evenings.

He lingered on the veranda a little longer than necessary and grew concern whenever he doesn't see her.

He glanced toward the road more often than his laptop deserved.

He found himself hoping to hear footsteps before he heard them.

Cynthia, for her part, began timing her morning walks with remarkable consistency.

If anyone had asked, she would have insisted it was coincidence.

Joseph would have said the same.

Neither of them would have been telling the complete truth.

Chapter Four

The Distance Between Us

There comes a moment in certain friendships that no calendar records.

No date announces it.

No conversation confirms it.

One day, the word "friend" simply begins to feel too small for everything it is trying to hold.

Neither Joseph nor Cynthia noticed exactly when that moment arrived.

Or perhaps they noticed and quietly chose not to name it.

By then, they had exchanged phone numbers.

Conversations that once belonged to the space between their compounds now stretched late into the night. A discussion that began on the veranda often continued through text messages long after both of them had gone indoors.

They shared more than conversations.

They shared recommendations.

Books.

Movies.

Songs.

Dreams.

When Cynthia admitted she was curious about programming, Joseph sent her beginner-friendly resources, insisting she didn't have to become a software developer to appreciate how technology and computers worked.

She laughed.

"I already have enough to study."

"You asked," he replied.

"I know."

"And I'll probably never stop asking."

He smiled.

"I figured."

One Saturday afternoon, Joseph's cousin invited Cynthia over while everyone sat outside enjoying the cool evening breeze.

The invitation was casual.

Her acceptance was just as effortless.

Hours slipped by unnoticed.

They spoke about music and technology, about purpose and ambition, about whether intelligence was something people were born with or something they built over time.

Joseph found himself listening as much as he spoke. They were learning from each other.

Cynthia had a way of asking questions that made ordinary thoughts seem worth exploring.

She challenged ideas without challenging people.

It was one of the things he admired most about her.

There were moments — small enough to seem insignificant, yet impossible to forget.

One evening, while excitedly explaining the story behind a song she loved, Cynthia became so animated that she reached across and grabbed Joseph's forearm to emphasize her point.

Neither of them acknowledged it.

Neither of them moved away.

Another afternoon, Joseph helped fix an issue with her laptop and helped her update it from Windows 10 to Windows 11 before she left for a rehearsal.

When it finally powered on properly, she looked at him with a smile that lingered just a second longer than gratitude usually required.

He noticed.

He pretended he hadn't.

She noticed that too.

Joseph had spent six years convincing himself that being alone was easier.

Not because he no longer believed in love.

Because he had quietly stopped believing it would find him again. He had always felt like he wasn't handsome enough to attract the female gender and too lazy or shy to walk up and initiate a conversation with one.

Without realizing it, he had built walls around parts of himself that once welcomed people in.

Now those walls were beginning to crack.

Not because someone forced them open.

Because someone had patiently given him a reason to unlock the gate from the inside.

Cynthia sensed the change as well.

She was perceptive enough to recognize that the feeling she experienced whenever Joseph's name appeared on her phone was no longer ordinary.

Yet she also knew she wasn't rushing toward anything.

University was about to begin.

Music demanded discipline.

Her dreams demanded sacrifice.

She had promised herself that she would never abandon those dreams — not for fear, not for comfort, and not even for love.

Still...

She found herself lingering a little longer whenever they spoke.

She timed her walks without admitting she was timing them.

She replied to his messages almost immediately...

then waited a minute before pressing "Send", hoping it might make her seem just a little less eager.

It fooled no one.

Least of all herself.

A week later, Cynthia travelled to attend a relative's convocation ceremony.

The distance between them amounted to only a few hours on the road.

Yet it somehow felt much farther.

They spoke every day.

Sometimes by text.

Sometimes through long phone calls that wandered from one subject to another without either of them noticing how much time had passed.

Joseph smiled at his phone more often than he cared to admit.

Cynthia found herself checking every notification with quiet hope, disappointed only when it wasn't his name lighting up the screen.

One evening, somewhere between jokes and ordinary conversation, Joseph said something he hadn't intended to say. Cynthia misheard him and asked him to repeat himself. He refused. She persuaded him and finally, after much pressure and convincing, he succumbed.

"I miss your presence around here."

Silence.

Not an uncomfortable silence.

A surprised one.

Cynthia lowered her eyes, smiling to herself even though he couldn't see it.

"I'll be home next week," she said softly.

"I know."

"I'm looking forward to seeing you."

"So am I."

After that, she kept him updated throughout her journey home.

Not because he asked.

Because she wanted him to know.

The feeling between them continued to grow the way the truest feelings often do...

quietly,

patiently,

without demanding to be noticed.

Until one evening, sitting together beneath the warm glow of the veranda light, Joseph broke the silence.

"I'm glad I moved here."

Cynthia looked at him.

"Why?"

He hesitated.

Not because he didn't know the answer.

Because he knew exactly what the answer meant.

When he finally met her eyes, she understood before he spoke.

She didn't look away.

What followed was not a dramatic confession.

There were no rehearsed speeches.

No grand declarations.

Only two young people choosing honesty over fear.

They admitted what had been quietly growing between them.

They admitted they were afraid.

And then...

they admitted they wanted to try anyway.

Chapter Five

The Weight of Almost Giving Up

You might think everything became perfect after that.

It didn't.

Love is not a reward that exempts two people from life's difficulties.

If anything, it asks them to face those difficulties together.

For a while, everything felt wonderfully new.

Their relationship gave ordinary days a different texture. Morning messages became something to look forward to. Evening conversations lingered longer than either of them intended. Even silence, when they shared it, felt comfortable.

Joseph laughed more.

Cynthia smiled more.

Even Joseph's cousin quietly noticed the difference, smiling to himself whenever Joseph lingered on the veranda long after there was any practical reason to remain there.

But life rarely waits for happiness before introducing another challenge.

University began.

For Cynthia, it was everything she had dreamed of.

And everything she had underestimated.

The Music programme demanded more than talent. It demanded discipline, rehearsals, assignments, performances, countless hours of practice, and the emotional energy to keep creating even when exhaustion whispered otherwise.

Days became longer.

Weeks became shorter.

Sometimes she returned home so mentally and physically drained that even holding a conversation felt difficult.

She wasn't withdrawing from Joseph.

She was simply trying to keep up with the life she had worked so hard to earn.

Joseph understood that.

At least...

the rational part of him did.

The quieter, more fragile part — the one that had spent six years expecting loneliness to return without warning — began to tell a different story.

Her replies became shorter.

Their conversations became less frequent.

The silence started filling itself with questions he never asked aloud.

"Was she losing interest?"...

"Had something changed?"...

"Was he slowly becoming less important?"

]He hated those thoughts.

He knew they weren't fair.

Yet fear has a way of sounding convincing when it speaks in the middle of the night.

Without realizing it, Joseph began pulling away.

He told himself he was giving her space.

Partly, he was.

But another part of him was retreating before disappointment had the chance to arrive.

Cynthia noticed.

She noticed because she always noticed.

She was exhausted from lectures, rehearsals, vocal training, and preparing for an upcoming recital that could open doors she had dreamed about for years.

Yet even in the middle of that demanding season, she could tell something had changed.

Joseph still cared.

She could hear it in his voice.

But something inside him had become distant.

One evening, she asked gently,

"Is everything okay?"

Joseph smiled.

"I'm fine."

It was the kind of answer people give when they don't yet know how to explain what they're feeling.

She accepted it.

Not because she believed it...

but because she respected that everyone arrives at honesty in their own time.

Days passed.

Misunderstandings multiplied.

Conversations that should have lasted ten minutes ended after two.

Messages were read, then answered hours later — not out of neglect, but because life refused to slow down.

Neither of them wanted to hurt the other.

Yet both of them quietly wondered whether they already had.

Cynthia worried she wasn't giving Joseph enough.

Not enough time.

Not enough attention.

Not enough of herself.

She had carried that fear before.

She remembered what it felt like to believe she had failed someone simply because life demanded more from her than she could give.

Joseph wrestled with a different fear.

He wondered whether loving someone with dreams this big meant learning to accept long seasons of absence.

He wondered whether six years of protecting himself had quietly left him unsure how to love without fear.

Neither of them said these things.

Instead...

they loved each other silently while misunderstanding each other completely.

The hardest night arrived without warning.

It was a Sunday.

Cynthia had promised to call after finishing her assignments.

She meant to.

She truly did.

But sometime after opening her notebook, exhaustion overtook her.

She fell asleep at her desk.

When she woke, the room was dark.

Her phone lay beside her.

Three missed calls.

Her heart dropped.

She called immediately.

Joseph answered.

His voice was calm.

Too calm.

The conversation that followed wasn't loud.

There were no raised voices.

No accusations.

Only two people speaking carefully while trying not to reveal how deeply afraid they both were.

When the call ended, nothing had been resolved.

Cynthia cried quietly into her pillow.

Finding it hard to breathe. Her mind racing through different thoughts at the same time.

Joseph sat alone in the darkness, listening to every insecurity he had spent years trying to silence.

A familiar voice whispered inside him.

"See?"

"This is how it ends."

"Walk away now before losing her hurts even more."

He didn't sleep that night.

Neither did she.

Chapter Six

Choosing Love Again

The next morning, Joseph made a decision.

he would not let fear make his choice for him.

The previous night had been long. Sleep had never really come. Instead, he had spent the quiet hours replaying every conversation, every smile, every shared laugh, and every moment that had brought them to where they were.

Somewhere between midnight and dawn, he realized something.

His fear had been speaking louder than the truth.

The truth was not the missed phone call.

The truth was Cynthia laughing at most of his jokes even the terrible ones.

It was the way she listened whenever he spoke about technology, even when she didn't fully understand it.

It was the way she remembered the smallest details about his day.

It was every ordinary moment that had quietly become extraordinary simply because she had been part of it.

One missed call could not erase all of that.

So, the following morning, he called her.

She answered before the second ring had finished.

Neither of them sounded as though they had slept.

"Good morning," Joseph said softly.

"Good morning."

For a brief moment, neither of them spoke.

Then Joseph took a slow breath.

"I'm sorry."

Cynthia closed her eyes.

"So am I."

He shook his head, even though she couldn't see him.

"I'm not apologizing for being hurt," he said. "I'm apologizing for hiding it. I should have told you what I was feeling instead of pretending everything was okay."

Silence.

Gentle silence.

Then Cynthia spoke.

"I'm sorry I missed your calls. I wasn't ignoring you. I fell asleep while studying, and when I woke up..." Her voice faltered. "...I hated knowing I'd hurt you."

"I know."

"I would never choose to hurt you, Joseph."

"I know."

There was another pause.

This time, Joseph chose honesty.

"I get scared."

She waited.

"When I'm afraid of losing someone, I become quiet. I tell myself I'm protecting my heart, but all I'm really doing is pushing people away."

For a long moment, Cynthia said nothing.

When she finally spoke, her voice was almost a whisper.

"I do the same thing."

Joseph blinked.

"When life becomes overwhelming," she continued, "I disappear into myself. I convince myself I'll explain later, but sometimes 'later' comes too late."

Neither of them tried to fix the other.

Neither of them offered perfect answers.

Instead...

they understood.

The conversations that followed over the next few days were unlike any they had shared before.

They spoke honestly about fear.

About loneliness.

About expectations.

Joseph told her what six years of emotional solitude had done to the way he interpreted silence.

Cynthia spoke about the quiet scars left behind by her previous relationships and how they had taught her to carry more burdens alone than she should.

For the first time, they weren't simply learning about each other's dreams.

They were learning about each other's wounds.

And somehow, that made the dreams feel even safer.

They made small changes.

Not dramatic ones.

Intentional ones.

Cynthia began setting reminders on particularly demanding days — not because she might forget Joseph, but because she wanted her actions to reflect how much he mattered.

Joseph made a promise of his own.

Whenever fear crept into his mind, he would no longer disappear behind silence.

Instead, he would say four simple words.

"I'm in my head."

Four ordinary words.

Yet they became a bridge between them.

The months that followed were not free from disagreements.

They still misunderstood one another from time to time.

They still argued.

Sometimes they needed space.

Sometimes they needed patience.

But silence no longer became punishment.

Conversations replaced assumptions.

Understanding replaced imagination.

Little by little, they stopped fighting each other...

and began fighting for each other.

Joseph watched Cynthia pour herself into rehearsals, performances, and countless hours of practice.

The more he witnessed her discipline, the more he admired the woman she was becoming.

He stopped seeing her busy schedule as something competing with their relationship.

Instead, he saw it as part of the future they both hoped to build.

Cynthia watched Joseph grow as well.

Late nights became completed projects.

Completed projects became confidence.

Confidence became opportunity.

She celebrated every small victory with him as though it were her own.

Neither of them became perfect.

Neither of them expected perfection.

What they built was something quieter.

Something steadier.

Trust.

Not the kind that appears overnight.

The kind that is earned, one honest conversation at a time.

Years later, neither of them would remember every disagreement.

They wouldn't remember every missed phone call or every misunderstanding.

But they would remember this.

The morning they both chose love over fear.

And how that single decision became the beginning of everything that followed.

Chapter Seven

The Beginning Hidden in Forever

Every love story reachs a moment when the pages begin to runout.

Not because the love has ended or has began to fade into the sunset.

But because words eventually become too small for everything the heart wishes to say.

Joseph and Cynthia continued building a life shaped by ordinary moments.

They celebrated birthdays.

Cheered each other's victories.

Comforted one another through disappointments.

There were still disagreements.

Still difficult days.

Still moments when life tested the promises they had made beneath the soft glow of a veranda light.

But they had learned something that many people spend a lifetime trying to understand.

Love was never the absence of conflict.

Love was the willingness to remain present while working through it.

Joseph watched Cynthia grow into the musician she had always dreamed of becoming.

The timid, and shy young woman who had once carried her dreams quietly now stood before audiences with confidence, allowing her voice to tell stories that words alone never could.

Every performance reminded him of the girl who had once spoken about those dreams with quiet hope.

She had become the woman she imagined.

Cynthia watched Joseph transform as well.

The uncertain graduate who had questioned his future became a confident software developer whose curiosity and persistence opened doors he had once believed were beyond his reach.

She had always believed he would find his path.

Seeing him walk it filled her with quiet pride.

People often asked them the secret behind their relationship.

They never had a complicated answer.

Sometimes Joseph simply smiled and said,

"We learned to talk before we learned to assume."

She usually laughed before adding,

"And we never stopped choosing each other."

Years passed.

The seasons changed.

The neighbourhood changed.

Even the veranda where so many conversations had begun eventually became only a memory.

Yet some memories never really leave us.

They simply become part of who we are.

Perhaps that is what love truly is.

Not a collection of grand gestures.

But thousands of ordinary moments that quietly shape extraordinary lives.

And so...

Joseph closed the notebook.

He smiled.

Then, slowly, he turned the page.

To Deborah

Dear Deborah ❤️

If you've made it this far, thank you.

You may have spent the last few hours getting to know two people named Joseph and Cynthia.

The truth is...

Joseph never existed.

Neither did Cynthia.

At least, not in the way stories usually introduce their characters.

Joseph was simply the name I gave a young man who was trying to find his place in the world.

His real name is.... Well, you sha know my name na except say na werey you be 😂.

Cynthia was the name I gave to a young woman whose kindness, strength, and quiet ambition inspired every page you have just read.

Her real name is..... Alfred Deborah from across 😁.

Not everything in this story happened exactly as written.

Some conversations were imagined.

Some moments were rearranged.

Some scenes existed only because they expressed emotions that ordinary words never could.

But the feelings behind them...

Those are real.

Every hope.

Every fear.

Every smile.

Every prayer.

Every dream.

Those belong to me.

When I told you that you were my greatest achievement since I arrived in Kubwa, I wasn't lying babe...

I wrote this story because I wanted to show you something that a simple message could never fully express.

I didn't want to tell you I admired you.

I wanted you to experience why.

I didn't want to say I believed in your dreams.

I wanted you to see someone believing in them through Joseph's eyes.

And I didn't want to promise that relationships are always easy.

I wanted to promise something more honest.

That if life ever places storms in our path, I hope we'll choose conversation over silence, understanding over assumptions, and patience over pride.

You may remember some moments that inspired these pages.

Others may feel entirely unfamiliar.

That's because this story isn't meant to be a record of our past.

It's a picture of the future I hope we might one day build together.

Whether that future ever becomes reality is something neither of us can know today.

Life has a way of writing chapters no one expects.

But if there is one thing I hope you carry with you after reading this, it is this:

You have already inspired something beautiful.

Not because of grand gestures.

Not because of extraordinary circumstances.

But simply by being yourself.

Thank you for reminding me that gentle hearts still exist.

Thank you for making me smile more than you probably realize.

Thank you for unknowingly becoming the inspiration behind a story that changed me while I was writing it.

Whatever tomorrow brings, I will always be grateful that our paths crossed.

With sincerity and love,

Samuel ✌
